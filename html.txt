<p id="mwGA">The <b id="mwGQ">dihydrogen monoxide parody</b> is a <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Parody" title="Parody" id="mwGg">parody</a> that involves referring to <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Water" title="Water" id="mwGw">water</a> by its unfamiliar <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Systematic_name#In_chemistry" title="Systematic name" id="mwHA">chemical systematic name</a> "<b id="mwHQ">dihydrogen monoxide</b>" (<b id="mwHg">DHMO</b>, or the <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula" id="mwHw">chemical formula</a> <b id="mwIA">H<sub id="mwIQ">2</sub>O</b>) and describing some <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Properties_of_water" title="Properties of water" id="mwIg">properties of water</a> in a particularly concerning manner — such as the ability to accelerate corrosion (<a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Rust" title="Rust" id="mwIw">rust</a>) and cause suffocation (<a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Drowning" title="Drowning" id="mwJA">drowning</a>) — for the purpose of encouraging <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Alarmism" title="Alarmism" id="mwJQ">alarmism</a> among the audience to often incite a <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Moral_panic" title="Moral panic" id="mwJg">moral panic</a> calling for water to be banned, regulated strictly or labeled as a <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Hazardous_chemical" title="Hazardous chemical" class="mw-redirect" id="mwJw">hazardous chemical</a>. Occasionally, reports also reference its widespread contamination of rivers or municipal water supplies. The parody has also involved other uncommon <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Chemical_nomenclature" title="Chemical nomenclature" id="mwKA">chemical nomenclatures</a> for water such as "hydrogen hydroxide", "dihydrogen oxide" and "hydric acid", used in many <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Prank" title="Prank" class="mw-redirect" id="mwKQ">prank</a> shows to scare people into thinking that it is a lethal or corrosive substance.</p>

<p id="mwKg">The motivation behind the parody is to play into <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Chemophobia" title="Chemophobia" id="mwKw">chemophobia</a>, and to demonstrate how exaggerated analysis, <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Information_overload" title="Information overload" id="mwLA">information overload</a> and a lack of <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Scientific_literacy" title="Scientific literacy" id="mwLQ">scientific literacy</a> can lead to misplaced fears.</p>

<meta property="mw:PageProp/toc" data-mw='{"autoGenerated":true}' id="mwLg"/></section><section data-mw-section-id="1" id="mwLw"><h2 id="History">History</h2>
<p id="mwMA">In 1983 on <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./April_Fools'_Day" title="April Fools' Day" id="mwMQ">April Fools' Day</a>, an edition of the <i id="mwMg"><a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Durand_Express" title="Durand Express" id="mwMw">Durand Express</a></i>, a weekly newspaper in <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Durand,_Michigan" title="Durand, Michigan" id="mwNA">Durand, Michigan</a>, reported that "dihydrogen oxide"<!--the source calls it "oxide"--> had been found in the city's water pipes, and warned that it was fatal if inhaled, and could produce blistering vapors.<sup about="#mwt11" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-Durand_1-0" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{"name":"Durand"}}'><a href="./Dihydrogen_monoxide_parody#cite_note-Durand-1" id="mwNQ"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwNg"><span class="cite-bracket" id="mwNw">[</span>1<span class="cite-bracket" id="mwOA">]</span></span></a></sup></p>

(September 2023)">citation needed</span></a></i><span typeof="mw:Entity">]</span></sup> This new version of the parody was created by housemates while attending UC Santa Cruz, in 1990, revised by Craig Jackson in 1994,<sup about="#mwt15" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-greatmoments_3-1" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{"name":"greatmoments"}}'><a href="./Dihydrogen_monoxide_parody#cite_note-greatmoments-3" id="mwRg"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwRw"><span class="cite-bracket" id="mwSA">[</span>3<span class="cite-bracket" id="mwSQ">]</span></span></a></sup> and was published in <i id="mwSg"><a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Analog_Science_Fiction_and_Fact" title="Analog Science Fiction and Fact" id="mwSw">Analog Science Fiction and Fact</a></i> in March 1996.<sup about="#mwt16" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-4" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{},"body":{"id":"mw-reference-text-cite_note-4"}}'><a href="./Dihydrogen_monoxide_parody#cite_note-4" id="mwTA"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwTQ"><span class="cite-bracket" id="mwTg">[</span>4<span class="cite-bracket" id="mwTw">]</span></span></a></sup> It received widespread public attention in 1997 when Nathan Zohner, a 14-year-old student, gathered petitions to ban "DHMO" as the basis of his science project, titled "How Gullible Are We?"<sup about="#mwt17" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-snopes_5-0" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{"name":"snopes"}}'><a href="./Dihydrogen_monoxide_parody#cite_note-snopes-5" id="mwUA"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwUQ"><span class="cite-bracket" id="mwUg">[</span>5<span class="cite-bracket" id="mwUw">]</span></span></a></sup></p>

</section><section data-mw-section-id="2" id="mwZg"><h2 id="Molecular_terminology_and_naming_conventions">Molecular terminology and naming conventions</h2>
<p id="mwZw">The water <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Molecule" title="Molecule" id="mwaA">molecule</a> has the <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Chemical_formula" title="Chemical formula" id="mwaQ">chemical formula</a> <link rel="mw-deduplicated-inline-style" href="mw-data:TemplateStyles:r1123817410" about="#mwt25" typeof="mw:Extension/templatestyles mw:Transclusion" data-mw='{"name":"templatestyles","attrs":{"src":"Module:Chem2/styles.css"},"parts":[{"template":{"target":{"wt":"chem2","href":"./Template:Chem2"},"params":{"1":{"wt":"H2O"}},"i":0}}]}' id="mwag"/><span class="chemf nowrap" about="#mwt25" id="mwaw">H<sub class="template-chem2-sub">2</sub>O</span>, meaning the molecule is composed of two <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen" id="mwbA">hydrogen</a> <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Atom" title="Atom" id="mwbQ">atoms</a> and one <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Oxygen" title="Oxygen" id="mwbg">oxygen</a> atom. Literally, the term "dihydrogen monoxide" means "two hydrogen, one oxygen": the prefix <i id="mwbw"><a rel="mw:WikiLink/Interwiki" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/di-" title="wikt:di-" class="extiw" id="mwcA">di-</a></i> in <i id="mwcQ">dihydrogen</i> means "two", the prefix <i id="mwcg"><a rel="mw:WikiLink/Interwiki" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/mono-" title="wikt:mono-" class="extiw" id="mwcw">mono-</a></i> in <i id="mwdA">monoxide</i> means "one", and "<a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Oxide" title="Oxide" id="mwdQ">oxide</a>" designates oxygen in a compound (the consecutive o's that would occur in "monooxide" are combined into one).<sup about="#mwt27" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-nomenclature_9-0" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{"name":"nomenclature"}}'><a href="./Dihydrogen_monoxide_parody#cite_note-nomenclature-9" id="mwdg"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwdw"><span class="cite-bracket" id="mweA">[</span>9<span class="cite-bracket" id="mweQ">]</span></span></a></sup></p>

<p id="mweg">Using <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Chemical_nomenclature" title="Chemical nomenclature" id="mwew">chemical nomenclature</a>, other names for water include: <i id="mwfA">hydrogen oxide</i>; <i id="mwfQ">hydrogen hydroxide</i>, which characterises it as a <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Base_(chemistry)" title="Base (chemistry)" id="mwfg">base</a>; and several designating it as an <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Acid" title="Acid" id="mwfw">acid</a>, such as <i id="mwgA">hydric acid</i> or <i id="mwgQ">hydroxyl acid</i>. The term used in the original text, <i id="mwgg">hydroxyl acid</i>, is a non-<a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Naming_acids#Nomenclature" title="Naming acids" class="mw-redirect" id="mwgw">standard</a> name.<sup about="#mwt28" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-About_H2O_10-0" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{"name":"About H2O"}}'><a href="./Dihydrogen_monoxide_parody#cite_note-About_H2O-10" id="mwhA"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwhQ"><span class="cite-bracket" id="mwhg">[</span>10<span class="cite-bracket" id="mwhw">]</span></span></a></sup></p>

<p id="mwiA">Under the 2005 revisions of <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./IUPAC_nomenclature_of_inorganic_chemistry" title="IUPAC nomenclature of inorganic chemistry" id="mwiQ">IUPAC nomenclature of inorganic chemistry</a>, there is no single correct name for every compound.<sup about="#mwt29" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-Nome_11-0" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{"name":"Nome"}}'><a href="./Dihydrogen_monoxide_parody#cite_note-Nome-11" id="mwig"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwiw"><span class="cite-bracket" id="mwjA">[</span>11<span class="cite-bracket" id="mwjQ">]</span></span></a></sup> The primary function of chemical nomenclature is to ensure that each name refers, unambiguously, to a single substance. It is considered less important to ensure that each substance should have a single unambiguous name, although the number of acceptable names is limited.<sup about="#mwt30" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-Nome_11-1" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{"name":"Nome"}}'><a href="./Dihydrogen_monoxide_parody#cite_note-Nome-11" id="mwjg"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwjw"><span class="cite-bracket" id="mwkA">[</span>11<span class="cite-bracket" id="mwkQ">]</span></span></a></sup> <i id="mwkg">Water</i> is one acceptable name for this compound, even though it is neither a systematic nor an international name and is specific to just one <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./State_of_matter" title="State of matter" id="mwkw">phase</a> of the compound (its liquid form). The other IUPAC recommendation is <i id="mwlA">oxidane.</i><sup about="#mwt31" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-Leigh_12-0" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{"name":"Leigh"}}'><a href="./Dihydrogen_monoxide_parody#cite_note-Leigh-12" id="mwlQ"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwlg"><span class="cite-bracket" id="mwlw">[</span>12<span class="cite-bracket" id="mwmA">]</span></span></a></sup></p>

<li id="mwASc">In April 2013, as part of an <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./April_Fool's_Day" title="April Fool's Day" class="mw-redirect" id="mwASg">April Fools' Day</a> prank, two radio personalities at <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./WWGR" title="WWGR" id="mwASk">Gator Country 101.9</a>, a station in <a rel="mw:WikiLink" href="./Lee_County,_Florida" title="Lee County, Florida" id="mwASo">Lee County, Florida</a>, were suspended for a few days after telling listeners that dihydrogen monoxide was coming out of their water taps.<sup about="#mwt59" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-Atlantic_28-0" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{"name":"Atlantic"}}'><a href="./Dihydrogen_monoxide_parody#cite_note-Atlantic-28" id="mwASs"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwASw"><span class="cite-bracket" id="mwAS0">[</span>28<span class="cite-bracket" id="mwAS4">]</span></span></a></sup><sup about="#mwt60" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-Hoax_29-0" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{"name":"Hoax"}}'><a href="./Dihydrogen_monoxide_parody#cite_note-Hoax-29" id="mwAS8"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwATA"><span class="cite-bracket" id="mwATE">[</span>29<span class="cite-bracket" id="mwATI">]</span></span></a></sup> The prank resulted in several calls by consumers to the local utility company, necessitating that the company send out a press release stating that the water was safe.<sup about="#mwt61" class="mw-ref reference" id="cite_ref-Radio2_30-0" rel="dc:references" typeof="mw:Extension/ref" data-mw='{"name":"ref","attrs":{"name":"Radio2"}}'><a href="./Dihydrogen_monoxide_parody#cite_note-Radio2-30" id="mwATM"><span class="mw-reflink-text" id="mwATQ"><span class="cite-bracket" id="mwATU">[</span>30<span class="cite-bracket" id="mwATY">]</span></span></a></sup></li>
